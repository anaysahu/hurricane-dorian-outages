State-by-State Breakdown

Florida ~22,500 total workers
Florida Power & Light (FPL)~16,000
Duke Energy Florida~6,500

FPL pre-staged what it described as the largest restoration workforce in company history — 16,000 workers drawn from 34 states via mutual-aid agreements — in advance of Dorian. The storm tracked further north than originally feared, sparing the Florida peninsula from a direct hit, and most pre-staged resources were subsequently redeployed northward to assist Carolinas restoration. Duke Energy Florida mobilized ~6,500 workers for its service territory (central Florida), with similar forward-deployment and northward redeployment.

North Carolina ~9,000 total workers
Duke Energy Progress (NC primary)~5,000
Duke Energy Carolinas (shared NC/SC deployment)~4,000

North Carolina bore the brunt of Dorian's U.S. landfall. Cape Hatteras recorded Category 1 conditions on September 6; the Outer Banks, New Bern, and the Cape Fear region saw the most severe outages. Duke Energy reported approximately 9,000 total workers deployed across both Carolinas combined (Duke Energy Progress serving eastern/central NC; Duke Energy Carolinas covering western NC and northwestern SC). These crews included out-of-state mutual-aid workers who traveled from the Midwest and Northeast under the Edison Electric Institute's Emergency Assistance Program.

South Carolina ~6,000 total workers
Duke Energy Carolinas (shared deployment across Carolinas)~4,000
Dominion Energy South Carolina~2,000

Coastal South Carolina experienced direct tropical storm impacts. The Charleston metro area recorded over 130,000 customers without power at peak — among the highest totals in the state. Dominion Energy SC (formerly SCE&G) mobilized roughly 2,000 workers including mutual-aid crews; Duke Energy Carolinas crews operating along the NC/SC border contributed to restoration in northwestern SC as part of the combined ~9,000-worker Carolinas deployment.

Georgia ~3,500 total workers
Georgia Power~3,500

Georgia's impact was comparatively modest — primarily scattered outages in coastal counties (Camden, Glynn, Ware) from tropical moisture bands ahead of the storm's main track. Georgia Power deployed approximately 3,500 workers in a readiness posture, with resources available to assist the Carolinas after it became clear Georgia would not take a direct hit. No significant inland transmission damage was reported.

  • Georgia Power Media Statement — Dorian preparation and crew deployment

Virginia ~1,000+ workers
Dominion Energy Virginia~1,000+

Virginia's exposure was limited to the Hampton Roads area and the Eastern Shore, where tropical storm-force winds caused localized outages. Dominion Energy Virginia pre-staged over 1,000 workers internally; a significant portion of this pre-positioned capacity was deployed southward to assist North Carolina and South Carolina restoration under mutual-aid agreements, rather than being needed locally.

Methodology & Data Limitations

All crew counts are approximate figures drawn from utility press releases, media statements, and news coverage published during and immediately after Hurricane Dorian's U.S. passage (August 28 – September 14, 2019). Utility companies typically report "total workers" as a broad category that includes lineworkers, vegetation crews (tree trimmers), damage assessors, logistics and support staff, and contractors — not all of whom are directly engaged in line repair at any given moment.

Mutual-aid counts are often reported at the Carolinas level by Duke Energy (combining Duke Energy Progress and Duke Energy Carolinas), making state-level attribution approximate. The Florida figure (~16,000 FPL workers) is widely cited and corroborated across multiple sources; the Virginia figure is the least precisely documented and should be treated as a lower bound.

Outage time-series data used in the associated visualization is from the U.S. Department of Energy hourly outage tracking dataset (Aug–Sep 2019), field: max_rolling_avg.
